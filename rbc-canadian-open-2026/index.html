The Course — Osprey Valley North

A Doug Carrick parkland in the hills of Caledon: wide, inviting fairways that hide the real defense — deep bunkers and big, rolling greens that demand the right angle.

Built by Doug Carrick in the rolling hills of Caledon, Ontario — one of three Carrick courses at Osprey Valley — the North is a parkland that plays nothing like it looks. The fairways are wide and the tee shots inviting; the teeth are in the green complexes. Carrick's deep greenside bunkers and undulating putting surfaces put a premium on attacking pins from the right angle — and ahead of its 2025 debut, former Carrick associate Ian Andrew repositioned every bunker, rebuilt tees and shaved shortgrass runoffs to give it championship teeth. Plenty of elevation change rolls through both nines. Corey Conners summed it up after a 2025 round: “this is a championship venue … you've got to have every part of your game working.”

Generous off the tee
Fairways hit: #5 of 37 venues

Carrick's wide corridors invite the driver — Osprey is among the most fairway-findable courses on Tour. Ryan Fox: “if you're a yard in the rough, you still feel like you've got a chance.” But position isn't about avoiding rough here — it's about earning the angle.

The defense is the greens
Fairway-miss penalty: top-9 of 34

Ian Andrew repositioned every bunker in 2023 and added shortgrass runoffs; the surfaces are big and rolling. Rory McIlroy: “these greens are quite undulating … you really have to control your spin.” Miss the fairway and you lose the angle into a deep-bunkered, sloped green — that's the real cost.

Gettable when soft, demanding when firm
2025 field: -0.07 to par / hole

Soft, it's a birdie-fest — Fox won at −18 and the field ran red. Sam Burns on the weekend: “it was definitely a lot firmer … made the golf course much longer.” The number on the board swings hard with the moisture — and the forecast is the lever to watch.

What Osprey Rewards — Tee to Green

A gettable, off-the-tee course: wide fairways, receptive greens, bimodal approaches, and birdie-friendly par 5s — off the tee is the one distinctive edge.

Osprey Valley North is the inverse of a brute. Players find the short grass and hit greens at top-8-of-37 rates, the par 5s give up red numbers, and every par type plays at or below the Tour norm. So separation doesn't come from raw difficulty — it comes off the tee: relative to the rota, off the tee is the one skill Osprey rewards more than tour-typical. Length and accuracy roughly cancel, though — a long ball even de-risks the miss — so swing freely off the tee, take your wedges, and convert the 5s.

How the Fit is built — and why to trust it

Each player's DataGolf skills collapse to one Venue Fit (0–100 field percentile), weighted Driving 50 / Approach 30 / Around-green 15 / Putting 5 — driving-forward because Osprey's one distinctively-rewarded skill was off the tee and accuracy is the bigger lever than distance. So the Drive component is 60% accuracy / 40% off-the-tee, and driving distance is deliberately left out. The validation: we correlated each skill against how the field actually struck the ball (SG total minus putting, per round) at the 6 courses that play most like Osprey (TPC Deere Run (John Deere); East Lake (TOUR Champ); TPC Twin Cities (3M); Waialae (Sony)…). The target excludes putting on purpose — going-in putting predicts comp-course putting, not course fit.
